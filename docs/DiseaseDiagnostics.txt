POULTRY AND CATTLE DISEASE DIAGNOSTICS GUIDE
For Field Veterinarians and Animal Health Workers in India
Department of Animal Husbandry and Dairying — Advisory Reference

==============================================================
SECTION 1: INTRODUCTION TO FIELD DIAGNOSTICS
==============================================================

Accurate and timely diagnosis is the cornerstone of effective disease management in livestock and poultry. Field diagnostics involve integrating clinical signs, history, flock/herd data, post-mortem findings, and where necessary, laboratory confirmation. This guide is organized by animal species and disease system to assist animal health workers in reaching a working diagnosis quickly in field conditions.

Key Diagnostic Principles:
- Always consider the signalment: age, breed, production stage, vaccination history.
- Gather epidemiological data: mortality rate, morbidity rate, duration, feed/water changes.
- Conduct post-mortem examination on freshly dead birds or animals when possible.
- Submit appropriate samples for laboratory confirmation of tentative diagnosis.
- Differential diagnosis should always be listed before concluding on a single cause.

SECTION 2: POULTRY DISEASE DIAGNOSTICS
==============================================================

----------------------------
2.1 RESPIRATORY DISEASES
----------------------------

DISEASE: Newcastle Disease (Ranikhet Disease)
Species Affected: Chickens, turkeys, other poultry
Age Group: All ages; chicks highly susceptible
Clinical Signs:
  - Respiratory: gasping, sneezing, rattling sounds, nasal discharge
  - Nervous: torticollis, circling, paralysis of wings and legs, tremors
  - Drop in egg production; soft-shelled or misshapen eggs in layers
  - Greenish-watery diarrhea
  - Sudden high mortality in velogenic strains (up to 100%)
Post-mortem Findings:
  - Hemorrhages in proventriculus and intestines
  - Congestion of trachea, lungs
  - Cecal tonsils may be hemorrhagic
Differential Diagnosis: Infectious Bronchitis, Fowl Cholera, Avian Influenza
Confirmatory Tests: Hemagglutination Inhibition (HI) test, RT-PCR, virus isolation
Important Note: Newcastle Disease is a notifiable disease. Suspected outbreaks must be reported to the nearest veterinary authority immediately.

---

DISEASE: Infectious Bronchitis (IB)
Species Affected: Chickens only
Age Group: All ages; more severe in young chicks
Clinical Signs:
  - Respiratory distress, gasping, nasal discharge, conjunctivitis
  - Tracheal rales (crackling sounds)
  - In layers: significant drop in egg production; watery albumen, wrinkled eggs
  - In young chicks (under 3 weeks): mortality may reach 25-30%
Post-mortem Findings:
  - Caseous plugs in trachea and bronchi
  - Air sac lesions (cloudiness)
  - Kidney form: swollen pale kidneys with urate deposits (nephropathogenic strains)
Differential Diagnosis: Newcastle Disease, Mycoplasmosis, Aspergillosis
Confirmatory Tests: RT-PCR, ELISA serology, virus isolation

---

DISEASE: Mycoplasmosis (Chronic Respiratory Disease - CRD)
Causative Agent: Mycoplasma gallisepticum (MG), M. synoviae (MS)
Species Affected: Chickens, turkeys
Age Group: All ages; often subclinical until stress
Clinical Signs:
  - Nasal discharge, facial swelling, coughing, rales
  - Reduced feed intake and weight gain
  - Swollen hock joints (MS)
  - Drop in egg production in layers
Post-mortem Findings:
  - Airsacculitis (thickened, cloudy air sacs with caseous exudate)
  - Sinusitis
  - Perihepatitis and pericarditis when complicated with E. coli (CCRD)
Differential Diagnosis: Newcastle Disease, Infectious Bronchitis, Fowl Cholera
Confirmatory Tests: Serum Plate Agglutination (SPA) test, PCR, culture
Management Note: MG/MS is frequently exacerbated by concurrent viral infections or poor ventilation.

---

DISEASE: Avian Influenza (Bird Flu)
Species Affected: All poultry species; wild birds as reservoirs
Age Group: All ages
Clinical Signs (HPAI — Highly Pathogenic):
  - Sudden high mortality with few premonitory signs
  - Severe respiratory distress, cyanosis of comb and wattles (blue/purple discoloration)
  - Facial edema, hemorrhages in legs and feet
  - Neurological signs, profuse watery diarrhea
  - Complete cessation of egg production
Post-mortem Findings:
  - Severe hemorrhagic lesions throughout the gastrointestinal tract
  - Necrotic foci in liver, spleen, kidneys
  - Subcutaneous hemorrhages
CRITICAL: HPAI is a zoonotic disease and a notifiable disease. Do NOT enter affected premises without proper PPE. Report immediately to District Veterinary Officer.
Confirmatory Tests: RT-PCR (H5, H7 subtype testing), antigen detection, virus isolation at approved labs
Differential Diagnosis: Newcastle Disease (velogenic), Fowl Cholera (acute)

----------------------------
2.2 ENTERIC DISEASES
----------------------------

DISEASE: Coccidiosis
Causative Agent: Eimeria species (E. tenella, E. acervulina, E. maxima, E. necatrix, etc.)
Species Affected: Chickens, turkeys
Age Group: Broilers 2-8 weeks; layer pullets 4-12 weeks
Clinical Signs:
  - Bloody diarrhea (caecal form — E. tenella)
  - Pasty or mucoid diarrhea (intestinal forms)
  - Huddling, ruffled feathers, anorexia
  - Pale combs, weight loss, reduced feed conversion
  - High morbidity, variable mortality
Post-mortem Findings:
  - Caecal coccidiosis: distended ceca filled with blood or caseous cores
  - Intestinal coccidiosis: thickened intestinal wall, petechiae, orange-mucoid contents
Differential Diagnosis: Ulcerative Enteritis, Necrotic Enteritis, Salmonellosis
Confirmatory Tests: Fecal flotation (oocyst identification), mucosal scraping microscopy
Treatment: Amprolium, Sulfonamides, Toltrazuril as per withdrawal period guidelines
Prevention: Coccidiostats in feed, good litter management, vaccination (Paracox)

---

DISEASE: Necrotic Enteritis
Causative Agent: Clostridium perfringens Type A and C
Species Affected: Broilers, turkeys
Age Group: 2-6 weeks (broilers); 7-12 weeks (turkeys)
Predisposing Factors: High-protein diet, coccidiosis, mycotoxins, immune suppression
Clinical Signs:
  - Sudden death without premonitory signs (peracute)
  - Depression, reluctance to move, wet litter
  - Diarrhea may be dark brown and foul-smelling
Post-mortem Findings:
  - Small intestine ballooned, friable with brown-black necrotic mucosa
  - Gas-filled intestines
  - Pseudomembranous lining on intestinal mucosa
Differential Diagnosis: Coccidiosis (intestinal), Ulcerative Enteritis
Confirmatory Tests: Gram stain of intestinal contents (large Gram-positive rods), culture, toxin typing
Treatment: Amoxicillin, Bacitracin, Lincomycin, Tylosin (per sensitivity)

---

DISEASE: Salmonellosis (Pullorum Disease / Fowl Typhoid)
Causative Agent: Salmonella pullorum (Pullorum), S. gallinarum (Fowl Typhoid)
Species Affected: All poultry; chickens most susceptible
Age Group: Chicks under 3 weeks (Pullorum); mature birds (Fowl Typhoid)
Clinical Signs:
  - Pullorum: white pasty diarrhea, pasting of vent, huddling, high mortality in chicks
  - Fowl Typhoid: yellowish-green diarrhea, anemia, weight loss, sudden death
Post-mortem Findings:
  - Pullorum: enlarged liver and spleen with necrotic foci; unabsorbed yolk sac
  - Fowl Typhoid: swollen liver (bronze discoloration), spleen and kidneys enlarged
Differential Diagnosis: E. coli infection, Coccidiosis, Marek's Disease
Confirmatory Tests: Whole Blood Agglutination Test (WBAT), culture and sensitivity
Public Health Note: Salmonellosis has zoonotic significance. Proper hygiene after handling affected birds is essential.

----------------------------
2.3 VIRAL IMMUNOSUPPRESSIVE DISEASES
----------------------------

DISEASE: Infectious Bursal Disease (Gumboro Disease)
Causative Agent: Infectious Bursal Disease Virus (IBDV)
Species Affected: Chickens
Age Group: 3-6 weeks (most susceptible); immunosuppression at any age
Clinical Signs:
  - Sudden onset of depression, ruffled feathers, anorexia
  - White watery diarrhea, vent pecking
  - Trembling, inability to stand, dehydration
  - Mortality 5-40% depending on strain; classic vs. very virulent strains differ
Post-mortem Findings:
  - Bursa of Fabricius: initially swollen and edematous (day 3-4), then atrophied (after day 5)
  - Hemorrhages in thigh and breast muscles (pathognomonic)
  - Kidneys swollen with urate deposits
Differential Diagnosis: Marek's Disease, Newcastle Disease, Infectious Bronchitis (nephropathogenic)
Confirmatory Tests: Agar Gel Immunodiffusion (AGID), ELISA, histopathology of bursa

---

DISEASE: Marek's Disease
Causative Agent: Marek's Disease Virus (MDV — Herpesvirus)
Species Affected: Chickens
Age Group: 3-30 weeks; most losses between 8-20 weeks
Forms: Visceral, Neural, Ocular, Cutaneous
Clinical Signs:
  - Neural form: progressive paralysis of one or both legs ("splits position"), wing paralysis, neck twisting
  - Visceral form: weight loss, diarrhea, sudden death
  - Ocular form: irregular, grey iris; partial or complete blindness
Post-mortem Findings:
  - Enlarged peripheral nerves (sciatic, brachial plexus) — loss of cross-striation
  - Tumors in liver, spleen, gonads, kidneys, heart, lung
  - Bursa of Fabricius typically atrophied
Differential Diagnosis: Lymphoid Leukosis, Newcastle Disease (nervous form), Botulism
Confirmatory Tests: Histopathology, PCR
Prevention: Vaccination at hatch with HVT or Rispens vaccine; no treatment available

----------------------------
2.4 BACTERIAL DISEASES
----------------------------

DISEASE: Fowl Cholera
Causative Agent: Pasteurella multocida
Species Affected: All poultry; ducks and turkeys highly susceptible
Age Group: Mature birds (>16 weeks) more commonly affected
Clinical Signs:
  - Peracute: sudden death of best-conditioned birds
  - Acute: fever, depression, mucoid nasal discharge, greenish diarrhea, dyspnea
  - Chronic: swollen wattles (cheesy exudate), torticollis, arthritis
Post-mortem Findings:
  - Petechial hemorrhages on serosal surfaces and heart
  - Fibrinous pericarditis and perihepatitis
  - Necrotic foci in liver
Differential Diagnosis: Newcastle Disease, Avian Influenza, Erysipelas
Confirmatory Tests: Culture on blood agar (small, non-hemolytic colonies), sensitivity testing

---

DISEASE: Colibacillosis (Avian Colibacillosis)
Causative Agent: Pathogenic Escherichia coli (APEC)
Species Affected: All poultry
Age Group: All ages; chicks and young birds most susceptible
Clinical Signs:
  - Variable: depends on infection form
  - Omphalitis (yolk sac infection) in chicks: mushy chick syndrome, navel infection
  - Air sacculitis, pericarditis, perihepatitis in older birds
  - Salpingitis in layers: causing drop in egg production
Post-mortem Findings:
  - Fibrinous pericarditis, perihepatitis, air sacculitis (polyserositis)
  - Omphalitis: unabsorbed yolk, yellowish exudate
  - Swollen liver with fibrin coat
Differential Diagnosis: Mycoplasmosis, Fowl Cholera, Marek's Disease
Confirmatory Tests: Culture and antibiotic sensitivity testing

SECTION 3: CATTLE AND BUFFALO DISEASE DIAGNOSTICS
==============================================================

----------------------------
3.1 FOOT AND MOUTH DISEASE (FMD)
----------------------------

Causative Agent: FMD Virus (Aphthovirus) — Serotypes O, A, Asia-1 prevalent in India
Species Affected: Cattle, buffalo, pigs, sheep, goats; rarely humans
Age Group: All ages; calves more severely affected
Clinical Signs:
  - High fever (40-41°C) for 2-3 days
  - Excessive salivation with stringy saliva
  - Vesicles (fluid-filled blisters) on tongue, dental pad, lips, gums, feet (interdigital space, coronary band), teats
  - Vesicles rupture to form painful erosions — causes lameness and reluctance to walk
  - Drop in milk yield (50-95%)
  - Calves: myocarditis, sudden death ("tiger heart")
Post-mortem Findings:
  - Vesicular lesions in oral cavity and feet
  - "Tiger heart": pale streaks in cardiac muscle of calves
Differential Diagnosis: Bovine Viral Diarrhea (BVD), Rinderpest (eradicated), Malignant Catarrhal Fever
Confirmatory Tests: Complement Fixation Test (CFT), ELISA (antigen detection), RT-PCR
Important: FMD is a notifiable disease. Report immediately to veterinary authorities.

----------------------------
3.2 HAEMORRHAGIC SEPTICAEMIA (HS)
----------------------------

Causative Agent: Pasteurella multocida Type B:2 (and E:2)
Species Affected: Cattle and buffalo; buffalo more susceptible
Age Group: All ages; adult working animals at high risk
Seasonal Pattern: Peaks in monsoon season (June-September)
Clinical Signs:
  - Sudden onset of high fever (41-42°C)
  - Profuse salivation, nasal discharge
  - Hot, painful edematous swelling of head, neck, brisket (characteristic sign)
  - Severe respiratory distress, dyspnea
  - Death within 6-24 hours in peracute cases
Post-mortem Findings:
  - Gelatinous subcutaneous edema in throat, neck, brisket
  - Hemorrhages on serosal surfaces
  - Congested lungs
Differential Diagnosis: Anthrax, Blackquarter, Trypanosomosis
Confirmatory Tests: Culture of blood/tissues (before antibiotic treatment), serology
Treatment: Penicillin-Streptomycin combination (early treatment critical); anti-HS serum (prophylactic)
Prevention: Annual vaccination with HS vaccine before monsoon

----------------------------
3.3 BLACKQUARTER (BQ)
----------------------------

Causative Agent: Clostridium chauvoei
Species Affected: Cattle (cattle more than buffalo)
Age Group: 6 months to 2 years; well-nourished animals preferentially affected
Clinical Signs:
  - Sudden lameness; reluctance to bear weight
  - Hot, painful swelling on affected muscle groups (hip, thigh, shoulder, neck)
  - Swelling becomes cold, emphysematous (gas under skin — crackling sound on palpation)
  - High fever initially, then subnormal as condition deteriorates
  - Death within 12-48 hours in most cases
Post-mortem Findings:
  - Dark red to black muscle with gas bubbles (characteristic)
  - Rancid-butter smell from affected muscles
  - Serous effusion in body cavities
Differential Diagnosis: Haemorrhagic Septicaemia, Anthrax, Snake bite
Confirmatory Tests: Fluorescent Antibody Test (FAT) on muscle smears, anaerobic culture
Prevention: Annual vaccination with BQ or combined Clostridial vaccine

----------------------------
3.4 ANTHRAX
----------------------------

Causative Agent: Bacillus anthracis
Species Affected: All domestic animals; zoonotic (highly dangerous to humans)
Clinical Signs:
  - Peracute: sudden death, bloody discharge from natural orifices, no rigor mortis
  - Acute: high fever, excitement followed by depression, difficulty breathing, bloody diarrhea
WARNING: DO NOT perform post-mortem on suspected anthrax cases. Spores are highly resistant and contaminate the environment for decades.
Action: Report immediately to District Veterinary Officer. The carcass must be incinerated or deeply buried with quicklime. Anthrax is a notifiable disease and a zoonotic risk.
Confirmatory Tests: Blood smear (McFadyean's reaction — polychrome methylene blue staining showing capsule), culture in approved laboratory
Prevention: Annual vaccination with Sterne live spore vaccine in endemic areas

----------------------------
3.5 THEILERIOSIS (TROPICAL THEILERIOSIS)
----------------------------

Causative Agent: Theileria annulata
Vector: Hyalomma ticks (H. anatolicum, H. dromedarii)
Species Affected: Cattle (especially crossbreds and exotic breeds); buffalo
Age Group: All ages; adult crossbred cattle most susceptible
Seasonal Pattern: Peaks in summer (April-June) when tick activity is high
Clinical Signs:
  - High fever (41-42°C), persistent
  - Enlarged superficial lymph nodes (parotid, prescapular, prefemoral)
  - Pale or icteric mucous membranes (anemia, jaundice)
  - Nasal and ocular discharge; respiratory distress
  - Profuse watery diarrhea
  - Rapid weight loss, drop in milk yield
  - Petechiae on conjunctiva and vulvar mucosa
Post-mortem Findings:
  - Enlarged, pulpy spleen
  - Swollen, orange-yellow liver
  - "Corneal opacity" — white patches on lung surfaces
  - Enlarged lymph nodes with petechiae
Differential Diagnosis: Anaplasmosis, Babesiosis, Trypanosomosis, Haemorrhagic Septicaemia
Confirmatory Tests: Giemsa-stained blood smear (piroplasms in RBCs), lymph node biopsy (schizonts)
Treatment: Buparvaquone (Butalex) 2.5 mg/kg IM — most effective; Halofuginone as adjunct

----------------------------
3.6 FOOT ROT (INTERDIGITAL NECROBACILLOSIS)
----------------------------

Causative Agent: Fusobacterium necrophorum and Prevotella melaninogenica
Species Affected: Cattle, buffalo, sheep
Age Group: Adult animals
Predisposing Factors: Wet muddy conditions, wounds in interdigital space, nutritional deficiency
Clinical Signs:
  - Acute lameness (sudden onset)
  - Swelling of interdigital space extending to coronary band
  - Foul-smelling necrotic tissue between claws
  - Fever, reduced feed intake
Post-mortem/Clinical Examination:
  - Necrotic, malodorous lesion in interdigital space
  - May progress to tendon sheath, joint involvement if untreated
Differential Diagnosis: FMD, Digital Dermatitis, Laminitis, Foreign body injury
Confirmatory Tests: Clinical diagnosis; bacterial culture for complicated cases
Treatment: Parenteral antibiotics (Penicillin, Oxytetracycline, Sulfonamides); hoof trimming; topical antiseptics
Prevention: Good drainage, hoof baths (5% formalin or 10% zinc sulfate)

----------------------------
3.7 MASTITIS
----------------------------

Causative Agents: Staphylococcus aureus, Streptococcus agalactiae, E. coli, Klebsiella, others
Species Affected: Dairy cattle, buffalo, dairy goats
Mastitis Types:
  - Peracute: sudden onset, systemic illness, toxemia
  - Acute: swelling, pain, firmness of affected quarter; abnormal milk
  - Subacute: minor changes in milk (flakes, clots), mild swelling
  - Subclinical: no visible signs; detected by CMT or SCC
Clinical Signs:
  - Swollen, hot, painful quarter(s)
  - Abnormal milk: watery, with clots, flakes, blood, or pus
  - Reduced milk yield
  - In peracute: high fever, depression, complete cessation of milk, gangrene of quarter possible
California Mastitis Test (CMT): Simple field test — positive result (gel formation) indicates subclinical mastitis
Confirmatory Tests: Milk culture and sensitivity, Somatic Cell Count (SCC)
Treatment: Intramammary infusion of antibiotics as per sensitivity; systemic antibiotics for clinical cases
Prevention: Teat dipping post-milking, dry cow therapy, proper milking hygiene, culling chronically infected cows

SECTION 4: SMALL RUMINANT DISEASE DIAGNOSTICS (SHEEP AND GOATS)
==============================================================

----------------------------
4.1 PESTE DES PETITS RUMINANTS (PPR)
----------------------------

Causative Agent: PPR Virus (Morbillivirus)
Species Affected: Goats (more severely), sheep
Age Group: Young animals 3-18 months most affected
Clinical Signs:
  - High fever (40-41°C)
  - Profuse mucopurulent nasal and ocular discharge
  - Erosions in oral cavity (tongue, gums, palate)
  - Severe diarrhea (may be blood-stained)
  - Respiratory distress, pneumonia
  - Mortality 50-90% in naive populations
Post-mortem Findings:
  - Necrotic and erosive lesions in mouth and intestines
  - Zebra-striping of large intestine mucosa (characteristic)
  - Congestion and consolidation of lungs
Differential Diagnosis: Foot and Mouth Disease, Contagious Caprine Pleuropneumonia (CCPP)
Confirmatory Tests: AGID, ELISA, RT-PCR
Important: PPR is a notifiable disease. Significant mortality in small ruminants should prompt immediate reporting.

SECTION 5: DIAGNOSTIC SAMPLE COLLECTION GUIDELINES
==============================================================

5.1 General Principles:
  - Collect samples from recently dead or sick (untreated) animals
  - Use sterile containers and appropriate transport media
  - Label clearly: animal ID, farm details, date, suspected diagnosis
  - Maintain cold chain (4°C for most samples; -20°C for viral samples during long transit)
  - Submit samples with a completed clinical history form

5.2 Sample Types by Suspected Condition:

Respiratory Diseases (viral):
  - Tracheal/cloacal swabs in viral transport medium (VTM)
  - Fresh lung tissue (5x5 cm block) in separate sterile container
  - Paired serum samples (acute and convalescent, 2-3 weeks apart)

Enteric Diseases:
  - Fresh fecal sample (5-10 g) in clean container
  - Intestinal scraping or loop of affected intestine (tied at both ends)

Bacterial Diseases:
  - Blood (before antibiotic treatment) in EDTA and plain vacutainer
  - Affected organ tissues in plain sterile container (DO NOT add formalin for culture samples)
  - Pus/exudate from lesions in sterile swab

Parasitic Diseases:
  - Fresh fecal sample for flotation and McMaster count
  - Blood smear (thick and thin) for blood parasites — stain with Giemsa
  - Skin scraping in liquid paraffin for external parasites

Histopathology:
  - Fix tissues (1 cm thick) in 10% neutral buffered formalin
  - Submit multiple organ samples: liver, spleen, lung, kidney, intestine, brain

5.3 Key Laboratories for Diagnostic Confirmation in India:
  - IVRI (Indian Veterinary Research Institute), Bareilly, Uttar Pradesh
  - NRC on Equines, Hisar
  - NRCE, Pune (for equines)
  - State Veterinary Colleges and Diagnostic Laboratories
  - Regional Disease Diagnostic Laboratories (RDDL) under DAHD

SECTION 6: DIFFERENTIAL DIAGNOSIS QUICK REFERENCE — POULTRY
==============================================================

Sudden High Mortality (Poultry):
  - With nervous signs: Newcastle Disease (velogenic), Avian Influenza, Botulism
  - Without premonitory signs: Avian Influenza (HPAI), Fowl Cholera (peracute), Blackhead (turkeys)
  - With respiratory signs: Newcastle Disease, Infectious Bronchitis, Avian Influenza

Drop in Egg Production (Layers):
  - Newcastle Disease, Infectious Bronchitis, Egg Drop Syndrome-76 (EDS-76), Mycoplasmosis, Infectious Laryngotracheitis, nutritional deficiency

Diarrhea in Poultry:
  - Bloody: Coccidiosis (E. tenella), Necrotic Enteritis
  - Green watery: Newcastle Disease, Fowl Typhoid
  - White pasty (chicks): Pullorum Disease, Omphalitis

Paralysis in Chickens:
  - Leg paralysis (splits): Marek's Disease (neural), Vitamin B2 deficiency
  - Floppy neck: Botulism
  - Torticollis: Newcastle Disease (lentogenic/mesogenic), Marek's

==============================================================
SECTION 7: DIFFERENTIAL DIAGNOSIS QUICK REFERENCE — CATTLE
==============================================================

Sudden Death in Cattle:
  - Anthrax (bloody discharge from orifices, no rigor mortis)
  - Blackquarter (muscle emphysema in young well-fed cattle)
  - Haemorrhagic Septicaemia (throat edema)
  - Lightning strike (no lesions, multiple animals)

Lameness in Cattle:
  - Vesicles on feet: FMD
  - Interdigital necrosis: Foot Rot
  - Swollen joint: Traumatic arthritis, Joint ill (calves)
  - Multiple limbs: Laminitis

High Fever with Tick Infestation:
  - Theileriosis (lymph node enlargement)
  - Babesiosis (hemoglobinuria — red urine)
  - Anaplasmosis (anemia without hemoglobinuria)
  - Trypanosomosis (intermittent fever, progressive emaciation)

Respiratory Signs in Cattle:
  - Fever + nasal discharge: BRSV, BVD, IBR, Pasteurellosis
  - Throat/brisket edema: Haemorrhagic Septicaemia
  - Frothy bloat: Clover poisoning, Grain overload
