POULTRY AND LIVESTOCK FARMING BEST PRACTICES
A Practical Advisory Guide for Indian Farmers
Covering Broilers, Layers, Cattle, Buffalo, and Small Ruminants

==============================================================
SECTION 1: POULTRY FARM MANAGEMENT
==============================================================

----------------------------
1.1 BROILER FARM SETUP AND MANAGEMENT
----------------------------

HOUSING REQUIREMENTS:
  - Orientation: East-West orientation of sheds to minimize direct sunlight on side walls
  - Ventilation: Cross-ventilated or tunnel-ventilated design; 30 cubic feet per minute (CFM) per bird minimum in hot weather
  - Floor space: Minimum 0.5 sq. ft. per bird (conventional); 0.65-0.75 sq. ft. recommended in hot regions
  - Height: Minimum 8 feet at sides, 12-14 feet at ridge for conventional open-sided houses
  - Litter: 2-3 inches of dry litter material (rice husk, sawdust, chopped straw); avoid caking and wet patches

BROODING MANAGEMENT (0-7 days):
  - Pre-heat the brooder area 24-48 hours before chick arrival
  - Target temperature: 32-33°C under brooder at chick level on day 1
  - Reduce temperature by 2-3°C per week until 22-24°C (or ambient, whichever is higher)
  - Check chick behavior to adjust temperature:
    - Huddling under heater = too cold, increase heat
    - Spreading away from heater = too hot, reduce heat
    - Uniform spread with gentle activity = correct temperature
  - Provide adequate brooder ring space: 50-75 chicks per square meter in ring
  - Light: 23-24 hours on day 1-3; reduce to 20-18 hours by end of week 1

FEEDING PROGRAM:
  - Pre-starter (0-7 days): 22-23% crude protein (CP), 3000 kcal ME/kg; crumble form
  - Starter (8-21 days): 21-22% CP, 3050 kcal ME/kg; crumble/mini-pellet
  - Finisher (22-35 days): 19-20% CP, 3100 kcal ME/kg; pellet form
  - Withdrawal (last 7-10 days): Antibiotic-free feed for food safety compliance

KEY BROILER PERFORMANCE BENCHMARKS:
  - Day 7 weight: 170-190 g
  - Day 14 weight: 450-500 g
  - Day 21 weight: 900-1000 g
  - Day 28 weight: 1400-1500 g
  - Day 35 weight: 1900-2100 g
  - Day 42 weight: 2400-2700 g
  - Feed Conversion Ratio (FCR): 1.70-1.80 (target), below 1.90 is acceptable
  - Mortality target: below 4% for 42-day cycle
  - European Production Efficiency Factor (EPEF) = (Livability % x Average Daily Gain x 100) / (FCR x Age in days)
    Target EPEF: above 320

LITTER MANAGEMENT:
  - Maintain litter moisture below 30%; wet patches must be removed and replaced immediately
  - Turn litter twice weekly in first 2 weeks; twice daily near drinkers if needed
  - Wet litter causes: ammonia buildup, coccidiosis, pododermatitis (footpad lesions), breast blisters
  - Ammonia levels above 25 ppm reduce feed intake and growth; above 50 ppm causes respiratory and eye damage
  - Simple field test: hold litmus paper 15 cm above litter surface for 10 seconds

----------------------------
1.2 LAYER FARM MANAGEMENT
----------------------------

PULLET REARING (0-18 WEEKS):
  - Uniformity is the most important parameter in rearing — target 85%+ uniformity at 18 weeks
  - Uniformity formula: % of birds within 10% of flock average body weight
  - Body weight must meet breed standard at each weekly checkpoint
  - Skip grading: sort birds by weight at 4, 8, and 12 weeks to improve uniformity
  - Light Program (Rearing): 8-10 hours light during rearing to prevent premature lay

LIGHTING PROGRAM:
  - Pullets raised under decreasing or constant photoperiod (8-10 hours)
  - At 18-19 weeks: stimulate with increase to 14 hours light/day
  - Peak production: 16 hours light/day (maintained throughout production)
  - Light intensity: 10-20 lux during production; uniform distribution critical
  - Any interruption or reduction in light hours during production = drop in egg production

LAYING HEN NUTRITION:
  - Pre-lay (17-18 weeks): increase calcium to 2.5-3.0%
  - Peak production: 16-17% CP; 2800-2850 kcal ME/kg; 3.8-4.0% calcium; 0.35% available phosphorus
  - Post-peak: Adjust calcium upward (4.0-4.5%) as shell quality often decreases with age
  - Avoid deficiencies in: Vitamin D3, Vitamin K, Manganese, Zinc (all affect shell quality)

EGG PRODUCTION TARGETS (Hen-day, breed-dependent):
  - 25-30 weeks: 80-90% (peak phase)
  - 35 weeks: 90-95% (peak production)
  - 40-65 weeks: 85-90% sustained
  - 72 weeks: 70-75%
  - Persistency (ability to maintain production): target less than 0.5% weekly decline post-peak

LAYER HOUSE ENVIRONMENT:
  - Optimal temperature: 18-22°C (above 32°C significantly reduces production and shell quality)
  - Humidity: 60-75% relative humidity
  - Ventilation: critical to remove heat, ammonia, CO2, moisture

----------------------------
1.3 WATER QUALITY AND MANAGEMENT
----------------------------

Water quality is often overlooked but is critically important. Poor water quality reduces feed consumption, impairs vaccine efficacy (chlorine kills live vaccines), and acts as a vector for disease.

Ideal Water Parameters for Poultry:
  - pH: 6.5-8.0 (above 8.0 impairs acidified electrolytes and some medicines)
  - Total Dissolved Solids (TDS): below 1000 mg/L (above 3000 mg/L = problematic)
  - Hardness: below 200 mg/L as CaCO3 preferred
  - Coliform bacteria: zero in water at point of delivery
  - Chlorine residual: 0.5-3 ppm at drinker level (balance disinfection vs. palatability)

Water Treatment:
  - Chlorination: Sodium hypochlorite at 0.5-1 ppm free chlorine at drinker level
  - Acidification: Organic acids (citric, formic) to bring pH to 5.5-6.0 reduces bacterial growth
  - Do NOT chlorinate during live vaccine administration (chlorine inactivates live vaccines)
  - Flush water lines weekly; clean and disinfect drinkers daily

Water Line Biofilm:
  - Biofilms in water lines harbor Pseudomonas, E. coli, and Salmonella
  - Between flocks: flush with chlorine at 50-200 ppm for 24 hours, then rinse thoroughly

----------------------------
1.4 FEED QUALITY AND MYCOTOXIN MANAGEMENT
----------------------------

Mycotoxins are fungal toxins produced in grains and feed ingredients under improper storage conditions. They are a major cause of performance losses in Indian poultry farms.

Common Mycotoxins and Effects:
  - Aflatoxin (Aspergillus flavus): liver damage, immunosuppression, poor growth, jaundice
  - Ochratoxin: kidney damage, reduced egg production, immunosuppression
  - Trichothecenes (T-2, DON/Vomitoxin): oral lesions, feed refusal, enteritis, immunosuppression
  - Fumonisin: esophageal and liver lesions (more in pigs/horses; moderate in poultry)
  - Zearalenone: reproductive problems (more significant in swine)

Field Indicators of Mycotoxin Problem:
  - Sudden unexplained feed refusal
  - Performance decline without clinical disease
  - Poor response to vaccination (immunosuppression)
  - Elevated liver enzymes; jaundice on post-mortem
  - Wet droppings / diarrhea without infectious cause
  - Oral or esophageal lesions (T-2 toxicosis)

Prevention of Mycotoxins:
  - Source grains from reputable suppliers; request mycotoxin test certificates
  - Store feed in dry, cool conditions; moisture content below 12% for storage
  - Use feed within recommended shelf life; FIFO (First In, First Out) management
  - Add mycotoxin binders/adsorbents to feed (bentonite, HSCAS, MOS, activated charcoal)
  - Inspect feed bags for signs of moisture, mold, unusual smell before use

SECTION 2: CATTLE AND BUFFALO MANAGEMENT
==============================================================

----------------------------
2.1 DAIRY CATTLE HOUSING
----------------------------

LOOSE HOUSING SYSTEM (Recommended):
  - Provides free movement for animals in a covered pen
  - Separate areas: lying area (cubicles or open pack), feeding area, water trough area, milking parlour
  - Minimum space requirement: 15-20 sq. m. per adult cow including exercise area
  - Roof: must provide shade and protection from rain; maintain height of 3-4 m for ventilation
  - Flooring: non-slip concrete with rubber matting in lying areas; 2-3% slope for drainage
  - Orientation: North-South for optimal shade; East-West for hot regions with prevailing cross-breeze

VENTILATION:
  - Open ridge roof ventilation for natural airflow
  - Electric fans in ridge or at cow level in high-density units (greater than 300 cows)
  - Heat stress in cattle begins at temperature-humidity index (THI) above 72
  - At THI above 78: reduce feed intake, misting fans essential
  - Install shade structures in outdoor holding areas

WATER:
  - Dairy cows require 100-150 liters of fresh, clean water per day (peak lactation)
  - Water trough space: minimum 60 cm per cow in group housing
  - Check and clean troughs daily; ensure 24-hour access
  - Water temperature preference: 15-20°C; very cold water reduces intake

----------------------------
2.2 DAIRY COW NUTRITION
----------------------------

Dry Matter Intake (DMI) Guidelines:
  - Lactating cow: 3-3.5% of body weight per day as dry matter
  - Dry cow: 1.8-2.0% of body weight per day
  - A 500 kg cow at peak lactation needs 15-17 kg dry matter per day

BODY CONDITION SCORING (BCS):
  - Scale: 1 (emaciated) to 5 (obese)
  - Target at dry-off: 3.5
  - Target at calving: 3.5 (never above 4.0 — increases ketosis risk)
  - Target at peak lactation: 2.5-3.0
  - Loss of more than 1 BCS unit in early lactation = metabolic disease risk

Transition Cow Management (21 days before to 21 days after calving):
  - Pre-calving ration: reduce potassium (below 1.5% DM), feed acidogenic ration (DCAD negative)
  - Avoid excess calcium pre-calving (prevents milk fever)
  - Provide free-choice mineral mix including magnesium
  - Post-calving: rapidly increase energy-dense feed; monitor for ketosis (off-feed, weight loss, sweet/fruity breath)
  - Propylene glycol drenching as ketosis prevention protocol: 300-400 mL/day for first 5-7 days post-calving

Feed Ingredients and Nutritional Targets (Peak Lactating Cow, 20-30 L/day):
  - Crude Protein: 16-18% DM
  - Net Energy Lactation (NEL): 1.65-1.72 Mcal/kg DM
  - Neutral Detergent Fiber (NDF): minimum 28% DM (effective fiber for rumen health)
  - Starch: 24-26% DM (higher increases acidosis risk)
  - Calcium: 0.9-1.0% DM
  - Phosphorus: 0.38-0.40% DM
  - Rumen-Protected Bypass Fat: up to 3% DM for very high producers

Common Feed Ingredients (India):
  - Roughage: green fodder (berseem, napier, maize), hay, crop residues (paddy straw, wheat straw)
  - Concentrate: maize grain, sorghum, groundnut cake, cotton seed cake, sunflower cake, fish meal, mineral-vitamin premix
  - Silage: maize silage is excellent for dairy; ensure proper fermentation (pH 3.8-4.2; no visible mold)

SALT AND MINERAL SUPPLEMENTATION:
  - Provide mineral mixture at 50-70 g/cow/day
  - Mineral block (salt lick) as supplement, not replacement for ration minerals
  - Key minerals in India: Selenium, Zinc, Copper deficiency common in many regions — test blood/soil annually

----------------------------
2.3 REPRODUCTION MANAGEMENT
----------------------------

Key Reproduction Targets (Dairy Cattle):
  - Days to first estrus post-calving: less than 50 days
  - Days to first service post-calving: 60-85 days (voluntary waiting period)
  - Conception rate at first AI: 45-55% (exotic breeds lower; HF x Sahiwal crosses higher)
  - Calving interval target: 12-13 months
  - Days open (calving to conception): less than 100 days
  - Services per conception: less than 1.7

HEAT DETECTION:
  - Primary sign: cow stands to be mounted by other cows (standing heat, 4-16 hours)
  - Secondary signs: restlessness, mounting other cows, clear mucus discharge from vulva, tail head abrasions
  - Duration of standing heat: 6-18 hours average
  - Ovulation occurs 12-24 hours after end of standing heat

Optimal Insemination Timing:
  - "AM-PM rule": cows detected in heat in the morning should be inseminated in the afternoon; cows in heat in the evening should be inseminated the following morning
  - For synchronized programs: fixed-time AI at 72-80 hours after CIDR removal (consult AI technician)

Signs of Pregnancy:
  - Absence of return to heat after successful insemination
  - Rectal palpation by veterinarian at 30-45 days: most reliable
  - Ultrasonography: detects pregnancy at 25-28 days (most accurate early method)
  - Milk progesterone test: reliable at 21-24 days post-AI

Post-Calving Complications to Watch:
  - Retained Placenta: if placenta not expelled within 12 hours, call veterinarian
  - Metritis: fever, foul-smelling red/brown vaginal discharge, reduced milk, off-feed (3-7 days post-calving)
  - Uterine Prolapse: immediate emergency — call veterinarian; keep clean and moist

----------------------------
2.4 CALF REARING
----------------------------

COLOSTRUM MANAGEMENT (Most Critical Period):
  - Colostrum must be fed within 2 hours of birth (ideally within 30-60 minutes)
  - Volume: 2-3 liters in first feed; total 5-6 liters in first 24 hours
  - Quality check: Use colostrometer — Brix refractometer reading above 22% = good quality
  - If dam's colostrum is unavailable: use stored colostrum (frozen at -20°C, thaw in warm water — do not microwave)
  - Failure of passive transfer (FPT) is the leading cause of calf death in the first month of life

CALF FEEDING SCHEDULE (Liquid Phase, 0-8 weeks):
  - Days 1-3: Colostrum (4-6 liters/day)
  - Days 4-60: Whole milk or milk replacer (6-8% body weight per day, divided in 2-3 feedings)
  - Water: free choice from day 3 onwards (essential even when on liquid feed)
  - Calf starter: offer from week 1; gradually increase as calf begins to consume
  - Weaning: when calf is consuming 0.5-1.0 kg starter per day consistently (usually 6-8 weeks)

CALF HEALTH MANAGEMENT:
  - Navel dipping: dip umbilicus in 7% iodine solution immediately after birth; repeat twice daily for 3 days
  - Ear tagging: apply tag within 24 hours for records
  - Dehorning: disbudding at 2-4 weeks with caustic paste or electric disbudder (younger = less trauma)
  - Housing: individual hutches for first 4-6 weeks prevents cross-infection; clean and disinfect between calves

----------------------------
2.5 HEAT STRESS MANAGEMENT
----------------------------

Heat stress is a major productivity constraint in India, particularly from April to June and during humid monsoon months.

Symptoms of Heat Stress in Cattle:
  - Increased respiration rate (above 80 breaths/minute)
  - Open-mouth panting, drooling
  - Crowding near water troughs
  - Reduced feed intake (grass below 40% of normal intake at severe heat stress)
  - Reduced milk yield (5-25% reduction)
  - Reduced conception rates

Temperature-Humidity Index (THI) and Risk:
  - THI below 72: No heat stress
  - THI 72-79: Mild heat stress (performance begins to decline)
  - THI 80-89: Moderate heat stress (significant production losses)
  - THI above 90: Severe heat stress (health risk, mortality possible in weak animals)
  - THI formula: THI = 0.8 x temperature(°C) + [(RH% / 100) x (temperature(°C) - 14.4)] + 46.4

Management Strategies:
  - Shade: all outdoor areas must have shade; minimum 4-5 sq. m./cow
  - Sprinklers + fans: most effective combination; wet the animal (not just the air)
  - Feeding time adjustment: shift major concentrate feeding to cooler evening/night hours
  - Increase water availability and frequency of trough cleaning
  - Reduce animal density in pens
  - Apply soaking (wetting the back and flanks with water)

SECTION 3: SMALL RUMINANT (GOAT AND SHEEP) MANAGEMENT
==============================================================

----------------------------
3.1 GOAT MANAGEMENT
----------------------------

HOUSING:
  - Raised platform housing (slatted floor 1-1.5 m above ground): best for humid regions
  - Space requirement: 1.5-2 sq. m./adult goat; 0.75 sq. m./kid
  - Ensure dryness and ventilation; goats are highly susceptible to pneumonia in damp conditions

NUTRITION:
  - Goats are browsers by nature; prefer leaves, shrubs, and browse over grass
  - Supplementation with concentrate: 200-400 g/day for lactating does; 150-200 g/day for dry does
  - Minerals: goats have high copper requirement but are sensitive to excess molybdenum
  - Avoid bracken fern, oleander, lantana, and other toxic plants in grazing areas

BREEDING:
  - Age at first breeding: 15-18 months (body weight above 25 kg for most Indian breeds)
  - Doe: estrus cycle 18-21 days; standing heat 12-36 hours; breeding season September-February
  - Buck ratio: 1 buck per 25-30 does for natural service; check buck fertility before mating season

KIDDING MANAGEMENT:
  - Separate doe from flock 3-5 days before expected kidding date
  - At kidding: clear mucus from nostrils, ensure kid suckles within 30 minutes
  - Colostrum intake: 10% of kid body weight in first 24 hours
  - Disinfect navel with iodine immediately

COMMON MANAGEMENT PRACTICES:
  - Deworming: strategic deworming based on fecal egg count (FAMACHA scoring for Haemonchus)
  - Hoof trimming: every 3-4 months to prevent foot rot
  - Shearing (sheep): once to twice annually depending on breed

----------------------------
3.2 SHEEP MANAGEMENT
----------------------------

WOOL SHEEP (Merino, Rambouillet, crossbreds):
  - Shearing: once yearly (spring) for wool production; twice yearly for Deccani and similar breeds
  - Body weight at shearing: above 35 kg for optimal wool production
  - Greasy fleece weight: 2-4 kg for Indian crossbred; 5-8 kg for fine-wool breeds

MEAT SHEEP (Deccani, Bellary, Nellore, etc.):
  - Target marketing weight: 25-30 kg at 5-6 months
  - Creep feeding lambs: high-protein starter from 2-3 weeks of age
  - Castration of male lambs not required for slaughter before 5 months

SECTION 4: INTEGRATED PEST MANAGEMENT
==============================================================

----------------------------
4.1 EXTERNAL PARASITES
----------------------------

POULTRY EXTERNAL PARASITES:
  - Red Mite (Dermanyssus gallinae): nocturnal blood-sucker; hides in cracks during day; causes anemia, reduced production, restlessness; control by thorough shed disinfection with acaricides between flocks
  - Chicken Louse (Menacanthus stramineus): causes irritation, feather damage; treat with pyrethrin-based powder or spray
  - Northern Fowl Mite (Ornithonyssus sylviarum): lives on birds permanently; treat with acaricide dip or spray

CATTLE/BUFFALO EXTERNAL PARASITES:
  - Ticks: use acaricide dips or pour-ons; rotate chemical classes to prevent resistance
  - Mange Mites (Sarcoptes, Psoroptes): ivermectin injection 0.2 mg/kg; topical acaricides
  - Horn Flies and Biting Flies: insecticide ear tags; pour-on insecticides; control manure accumulation

ACARICIDE ROTATION SCHEDULE:
  To prevent resistance development, rotate between:
  - Organophosphates (Malathion, Diazinon)
  - Synthetic Pyrethroids (Cypermethrin, Deltamethrin)
  - Amidines (Amitraz)
  - Macrocyclic Lactones (Ivermectin, Doramectin)
  - Never use the same chemical class for more than 2-3 consecutive treatments

----------------------------
4.2 INTERNAL PARASITES
----------------------------

POULTRY INTERNAL PARASITES:
  - Roundworms (Ascaridia galli): most common; causes poor growth, pale yolk; treat with piperazine or levamisole
  - Tapeworms (Raillietina species): control by elimination of intermediate hosts (beetles, flies)
  - Heterakis gallinarum (cecal worm): vector for Histomonas (Blackhead in turkeys)
  - Treatment: periodic deworming every 2-3 months in backyard flocks; for commercial flocks, follow manufacturer rotation schedule

CATTLE INTERNAL PARASITES:
  - Gastrointestinal nematodes (Haemonchus, Ostertagia, Cooperia): bottle jaw, anemia, diarrhea, poor growth
  - Liver flukes (Fasciola hepatica): affects liver, bile ducts; related to wet pastures and snail habitats
  - Tapeworms (Moniezia): calves most affected

Deworming Program for Cattle:
  - Adults: 2-3 times per year (before monsoon, peak of monsoon, post-monsoon)
  - Calves: every 2 months from 1 month of age until 12 months
  - Rotate anthelmintic classes:
    - Benzimidazoles (Albendazole, Fenbendazole)
    - Levamisole/Tetramisole
    - Macrocyclic Lactones (Ivermectin, Doramectin)
  - Perform fecal egg count (FEC) monitoring to guide treatment decisions

Liver Fluke Control:
  - Treat with Triclabendazole or Oxyclozanide
  - Control snail intermediate host by drainage of wet areas; copper sulfate application
  - Strategic treatment: post-monsoon (October-November) and pre-monsoon (March-April)

SECTION 5: FARM ECONOMICS AND PRODUCTIVITY METRICS
==============================================================

----------------------------
5.1 BROILER ECONOMICS
----------------------------

Cost of Production Components (typical):
  - Feed: 65-70% of total cost
  - Chick cost: 15-18%
  - Medicines and vaccines: 3-5%
  - Labor: 5-7%
  - Power, litter, miscellaneous: 3-5%

Key Performance Indicators (KPIs):
  - Feed Conversion Ratio (FCR): ratio of feed consumed to body weight gained; lower is better
    Target: 1.70-1.80 for 42-day birds
  - Weight Gain per Day: target 55-65 g/day average
  - Cost per kg live weight: benchmark against local market price; aim for 25-35% margin
  - Mortality: every 1% above target = loss of 1 bird per 100; factor into break-even analysis

----------------------------
5.2 LAYER ECONOMICS
----------------------------

Cost Distribution (per 100 birds/year):
  - Feed: 70-75%
  - Pullet cost: 8-12%
  - Health costs: 3-5%
  - Labor, utilities, depreciation: 10-15%

Break-even Egg Price:
  - Calculate cost per egg = total annual cost / total eggs produced
  - Monitor weekly against local market egg price
  - At break-even: no profit, no loss; target minimum 20-25% margin

Flock Profitability Drivers:
  - High hen-day production (above 85% in peak)
  - Low FCR (feed per egg): target below 140 g/egg
  - Low mortality (below 8% over 72-week cycle)
  - Good shell quality and egg weight (above 55 g preferred by market)

----------------------------
5.3 DAIRY FARM ECONOMICS
----------------------------

Revenue Sources:
  - Milk sales (primary)
  - Calf sales (male calves, surplus female calves)
  - Culled cow sales
  - Dung/biogas value

Cost Components:
  - Feed (green fodder, dry fodder, concentrate): 60-70% of total cost
  - Labor: 15-20%
  - Veterinary and medicines: 3-5%
  - Loan/interest on cattle purchase: 5-10%

Key Metrics:
  - Cost of milk production per liter (CPM): target 15-20% below farm gate price
  - Milk yield per lactation: target 3000-3500 L for HF crossbred; 2000-2500 L for Jersey crossbred
  - Calving interval: every extra month beyond 13 months = 1 lost calf and 1 month less peak milk

Break-even Analysis Example (per cow):
  - Annual cost: Rs. 60,000-80,000 (varies by region and management level)
  - Break-even production at Rs. 35/liter: 1,714-2,285 L/year
  - Profit at 3,000 L/year and Rs. 35/liter: Rs. 25,000-45,000 per cow per year (before capital costs)

SECTION 6: GOVERNMENT SCHEMES AND SUPPORT FOR FARMERS
==============================================================

----------------------------
6.1 KEY SCHEMES AVAILABLE TO POULTRY AND LIVESTOCK FARMERS (INDIA)
----------------------------

LIVESTOCK INSURANCE SCHEME (PMFBY - Animal Component):
  - Provides insurance coverage for cattle, buffalo, sheep, goat, pigs, and poultry
  - Premium: subsidized by Central and State Governments (farmer pays 15-30% of premium)
  - Coverage: market value of insured animal at time of death
  - How to apply: through nearest bank, cooperative, or District Animal Husbandry office

NATIONAL LIVESTOCK MISSION (NLM):
  - Covers entrepreneurship development in poultry, small ruminants, pigs, and feed
  - Provides financial assistance (subsidy/credit-linked) for setting up:
    - Broiler and layer farms
    - Hatcheries
    - Feed processing units
    - Breeding units for goat, sheep, pig
  - Applies to: SC/ST, BPL, and other categories at different subsidy rates

RASHTRIYA GOKUL MISSION (RGM):
  - Focus on development and conservation of indigenous cattle and buffalo breeds
  - Covers: Gokul Grams (integrated development centers), semen stations, AI infrastructure
  - Benefit to farmers: improved AI services, subsidized semen doses, bull mother farm support

KISAN CREDIT CARD (KCC) — ANIMAL HUSBANDRY EXTENSION:
  - Extended to animal husbandry and fisheries farmers (from 2019)
  - Covers: working capital for feed, medicine, hiring of labor
  - Interest: 7% per annum; 3% interest subvention for timely repayment (effective rate 4%)
  - How to apply: through banks (NABARD-linked commercial, cooperative, RRBs)

DAIRY ENTREPRENEURSHIP DEVELOPMENT SCHEME (DEDS):
  - Promotes modern dairy farms of 2-30 cows/buffaloes
  - Back-ended capital subsidy of 25% for general; 33.33% for SC/ST
  - Covers: purchase of milch animals, infrastructure (shed, equipment), cold chain

POULTRY VENTURE CAPITAL FUND:
  - For establishing broiler farms, layer farms, hatcheries, feed plants
  - Credit-linked subsidy through banks
  - Contact: NABARD or District Lead Bank Manager

----------------------------
6.2 EXTENSION AND ADVISORY SERVICES
----------------------------

KISAN CALL CENTRE (KCC):

  - Available 6 AM to 10 PM, all days

KRISHI VIGYAN KENDRAS (KVKs):

E-PASHUHAAT PORTAL (www.epashuhaat.gov.in):

NABARD ANIMAL HUSBANDRY SUPPORT:
  - Contact NABARD District Development Manager for dairy/poultry project financing
  - NABARD provides refinance to banks for animal husbandry loans

SECTION 7: SUSTAINABLE AND ORGANIC FARMING PRACTICES
==============================================================

----------------------------
7.1 INTEGRATING LIVESTOCK WITH CROPS
----------------------------

Livestock-crop integration reduces external input costs and builds a closed-loop nutrient cycle on the farm.

Benefits:
  - Manure from livestock = fertilizer for crops; reduces chemical fertilizer cost by 20-30%
  - Crop residues (wheat straw, paddy straw, maize stover) = dry fodder for cattle
  - Biogas from dung = cooking fuel for household + slurry as fertilizer
  - Poultry litter = high-nitrogen organic fertilizer (3% N, 3% P, 1.5% K)

BIOGAS PLANTS:
  - Recommended for farms with 5+ cattle
  - 1 kg dung/day from 1 cattle produces 0.023-0.040 cubic meters gas
  - A family of 4-5 needs 2-3 cubic meters gas/day for cooking (requires 6-8 cattle)
  - Biogas slurry (effluent) is an excellent liquid fertilizer; superior to raw dung
  - Schemes: New National Biogas and Organic Manure Programme (NNBOMP) provides subsidy

VERMICOMPOSTING:
  - Poultry litter + crop residues → vermicompost in 45-60 days
  - Earthworm species: Eisenia fetida (red wriggler) most efficient
  - Value: Rs. 6-12/kg for vermicompost; earthworm biomass also marketable

----------------------------
7.2 WATER CONSERVATION
----------------------------

  - Nipple drinkers in poultry save 30-50% water compared to open bell drinkers
  - Rainwater harvesting from shed roofs can supplement 40-60% of water needs in most Indian regions
  - Closed-loop manure handling (covered pits, biogas digesters) prevents groundwater contamination
